🎯 Key Takeaways

Energy drove the entire spike. Gasoline +21.2% m/m — the largest monthly increase since the BLS started the series in 1967. Fuel oil +30.7% m/m. Energy overall +10.9% m/m, the biggest jump since Hurricane Katrina (Sep 2005). This is the Iran war flowing through to the consumer.
Core inflation remains contained. Ex food & energy, CPI rose just 0.2% m/m — unchanged from February and below the 0.29% consensus. The Fed's worst nightmare (energy shock + broadening core inflation) hasn't materialized yet. Shelter +0.3%, services ex-energy +0.2%. Used cars -0.4%, medical care -0.2%.
Headline vs Core divergence is extreme. The 0.7pp gap between headline m/m (+0.9%) and core m/m (+0.2%) is one of the widest on record. This is a pure energy-driven print. The question: does it stay contained or does energy pass through to services and wages in coming months?

What This Print Means

Dovish lean in the data. Core at 0.2% m/m is benign — this is not the second-round effects the Fed fears. The energy shock is mechanical and (if the ceasefire holds) partially reversible. The Fed can look through a one-month energy spike. What they can't look through: if next month shows energy passthrough into services, wages, and inflation expectations.
The risk ahead: Tariffs are still pushing core goods higher (Goldman sees +0.22% m/m in core goods). Airline fares +14.9% y/y reflects jet fuel costs bleeding through. If oil stays above $90-100, the second-round effects will come — it's a question of when, not if.
🏆 Records & Context

🔴 Gasoline m/m: +21.2%

Largest monthly increase ever recorded in the gasoline CPI series (since 1967). Before seasonal adjustment: +24.9%. National average hit $4.02/gal — first time above $4 in 4 years.

🟠 Energy m/m: +10.9%

Largest since September 2005 (Hurricane Katrina). Fuel oil +30.7% — biggest since Feb 2000. Three-quarters of the all-items increase came from energy alone.

🔵 Headline m/m: +0.9%

Largest since June 2022. But headline y/y at 3.3% came in below the 3.4-3.7% consensus range — a small silver lining. The ceasefire (Apr 8) is NOT reflected in this data.
